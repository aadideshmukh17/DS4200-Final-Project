Food Security in America

An analysis of NHANES data across three survey periods: 2015–2016, 2017–2018, and 2021–2023

Introduction

Food insecurity — the limited or uncertain availability of nutritionally adequate and safe foods — is a persistent public health challenge in the United States. It affects millions of households across all demographics and is closely linked to negative health outcomes including chronic disease, developmental delays in children, and reduced quality of life.

This project analyzes data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES), a nationally representative survey conducted by the CDC. We examine three two-year survey waves spanning nearly a decade: 2015–2016, 2017–2018, and 2021–2023. This time span captures both pre-pandemic conditions and the significant disruptions — and policy responses — brought on by COVID-19.

Our four visualizations explore different facets of food insecurity: the overall distribution of food security status, how household food worry relates to benefit receipt, income and demographic patterns, and changes over time. Together, they aim to tell a fuller story of who experiences food insecurity and how that has changed.

Conclusion

Across three survey waves, the data reveal that food insecurity in American households is both widespread and persistent. While emergency benefit programs expanded significantly during the COVID-19 pandemic, the underlying rates of low and very low food security have remained stubbornly elevated. Households that express worry about running out of food consistently receive more months of assistance — confirming that self-reported worry is a meaningful indicator of genuine need.

Income remains one of the strongest predictors of food security status, and the data suggest that households near the poverty line are disproportionately vulnerable to food insecurity even when they participate in assistance programs. These findings point to the need for sustained, structural investment in food assistance beyond emergency expansions, as well as targeted outreach to households that are eligible for programs but not yet enrolled.

Data source: National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES), CDC/NCHS. Survey waves: 2015–2016, 2017–2018, 2021–2023. Food Security Questionnaire modules FSQ_I, FSQ_J, and FSQ_L.
